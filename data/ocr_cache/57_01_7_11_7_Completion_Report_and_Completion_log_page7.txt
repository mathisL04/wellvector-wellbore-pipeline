
B. ZECHSTEIN AGE DOLOMITE

B.1 ROCK DATA

RFT results in this zone (Append. 7) were a combination of seal failures and tight responses. Eventually the tool failed. Sidewall cores were shot and samples sent to the stimulation companies Dowell Schlumberger and Halliburton. Using the effective mud weight of 13.0 ppg (leak off test) at $10,599 \mathrm{ft} \mathrm{md}$ and a mudweight of 14.3 $\mathrm{ppg}$ at $14,960 \mathrm{ft} \mathrm{md}$ yields a bottom hole pressure of $11,000 \mathrm{psi}$ at the dolomite. This information coupled with the maximum allowable surface pressure of $8,000 \mathrm{psi}$ made a very difficult choice necessary. The stimulation wellhead pressure $\left(\mathrm{WHP}=\right.$ frac gradient $\mathrm{X}$ vertical depth $-$ hydraulic pressure $+$ friction pressure) exceeded the 8,000 psi limit. Permission was granted to uprate the allowable pressure to $8,800 \mathrm{psi}$ and the job was made more conservative in terms of volumes pumped. A lower fracture gradient was used based upon the belief that fracturing of the dolomite (as seen in this formation in UK waters) might be present. The dipmeter over the perforated interval (Fig. 9) is indicative of the possibility of some fracturing in the formation. No Fracture Indentification Log was run so it was impossible to say beforehand whether those fractures were actually there, or only present in the optimistic interpretation.

B.2 SERVICE COMPANY DESIGNS

Both Dowell Schlumberger and Haliburton's work on the sidewall cores indicated that the material was a dirty, calcareous, siltstone-limestone (Appends. 8 & 9). Both companies suggested stimulation procedures that might be succesful under these conditions if there were some fractures present to feed the near wellbore. They indicated low solubilities in $\mathrm{HCl}$ acid but high solubilities in HF acid. The problem with using HF is depth and pressure. The tubing would be too long in contact with the HF acid at this temperature $\left(350 \mathrm{deg} . \mathrm{F}\right)$ for the safety of the tubing. The HF could have conceivably released a large percentage of fines and for this zone there was a restricted amount of rathole. The predicted high formation pressure of $11,000 \mathrm{psi}$ with a maximum allowable wellhead surface pressure of $8,800 \mathrm{psi}$ virtually precluded the propped acid fracture stimulation recommended by both companies. Haliburton stated "The low acid solubility… may result in limited acid etching and the release of insoluable fines, which together will nullify the effect of creating a long fracture."
