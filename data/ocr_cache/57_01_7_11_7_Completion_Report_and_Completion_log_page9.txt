The GECO special core analysis (Append. 2) generated porosity, grain density, klinkenberg corrected air permeability, formation resistance, capillary pressure and confining pressure for the major zone of interest. Some of this data is plotted on Fig. 6 showing a fair comparison with the electric log generated values. The porosity ranges from $5.5 \%$ to $16.5 \%$ with a corresponding permeability of $.01$ to $1.9 \mathrm{md}$. The better permeability is associated with the better porosity which is a negative indicator for extensive fracture permeability. This report was not received until October, at which time the stimulation of the Smith Bank Fm. had already been attempted.

C.2 SERVICE COMPANY DESIGNS

Sidewall cores were sent to the stimulation companies. Early results of petrographic analyses (Append. 12) and chemical sensitivities indicated that there was alot of clay present as demonstrated on the CPI. Solubilities in $15 \%$ $\mathrm{HCl}$ ranged from $7-13 \%$ while $12-3$ (Mud acid $12 \%$ $\mathrm{HF}$ $3 \% \mathrm{HCl})$ solubilties ranged from $45-62 \%$. The PPCO sedimentary & petrography report (Append. 4) indicates that the clay percentage is low, less than $3 \%$ throughout the cored interval. This is in direct contrast to the CPI which averages about $30 \%$ clay content. The CPI gamma ray curve is very hot and may be the source of this discrepancy. The core study from Bartlesville further demonstrates that the better porosity is present in the Triassic due to porosity preservation and etching of feldspars. There is residual oil associated with the clays, but no oil staining in the sand itself. After analyzing the samples and receiving the well data sheet (Fig. 8), Dowell-Schlumberger and Halliburton put forth proposals for stimulating the Triassic zones. After the recent PPCO experience with injection rate problems in the Zechstein dolomite, particular attention was paid to minimum pumping rates specified for a successful operation. Additionally, a separate injection test was to be made using rig cementing pumps before calling for a stimulation vessel. Other go-nogo decision points were added to the procedure at critical points in committing resources.

As a this is a sandstone with limited carbonate cement, high pressure, and high temperature, the first design proposals were for massive proppant laden hydraulic fracture stimulations. Dowell-Schlumberger (Append. 13) was in favor of a two part job with a prefrac acid job followed by a hydraulic propped fracture, all run at a rate of $15 \mathrm{BPM}$. Halliburton (Append. 14) did not feel that the prefrac acid job was necessary, but did feel that the hydraulic job should be run at $20 \mathrm{BPM}$. The job was redesigned to include the prefrac acid treatment, several go- nogo decision points, and put out to bid.
