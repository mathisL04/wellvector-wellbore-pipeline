A.2 PROBLEMS

The well was scheduled as a 150 day project but a full drilling crew was still present at day 349 and it took until day 364 to set a plug above the last set of perforations. The length of this project was due to problems in drilling and extended testing to adequately evaluate the three zones of interest. Appendices 5 & 6 show respectively the program from the drilling point of view, and a time line of the operations. There were delays in setting plugs, running casing, fishing, and installing necessary 10,000 psi surface equipment. The testing delays include problems with setting DHSV's, parted tubing, aborted stimulations, bad weather, and bailing operations. The most important part of the year's work for Reservoir has been the attempted stimulation and testing of the three formations.

A.3 CHRONOLOGY

The Ula formation was encountered first and extensively cored as was the first 40 feet of the Smith Bank Fm. There were good shows and gas was seen in the drilling mud. The middle Triassic was less well sampled due to contamination by shale savings but 50 sidewall cores were shot (Append. 11). The lowest zone of interest, Zechstein age dolomite, did have reasonable returns and sidewall cores. As noted on the CPI (Append. 2) RFT's were taken throughout the zones of interest as defined by the electric logs. After discussion and review of the log data, five zones were designated for testing as delineated by their respective perforation intervals : Zechstein dolomite 15,914-15,946 ft md, Triassic zone 1 15,712- 15,679 ft md, Triassic zone 2 15,369- 15,320 ft md, Triassic zone 3 15,175- 15,242 ft md, Upper Triassic 15,007- 15,015 ft md, and Jurassic 14,925- 14,980 ft md. Due to pressure differences inferred from mud weights, completion string spacing requirements, and formation compositions, the testing was broken into three parts: Zechstein, Triassic 1,2,3, and Upper Triassic with Jurassic. Accordingly, samples were sent out in July and August for evaluation by the stimulation companies Dowell-Schlumberger and Halliburton.

The basic data as known at that time which was associated with those samples is shown in Figs. 7 & 8 along with some of the updated data in parentheses. Some of the updates came from the service companies' analyses while other information was obtained from the well bore during the testing operations. The most significant changes include the higher temperatures and pressures than anticipated with lower porosities and permeabilities.
