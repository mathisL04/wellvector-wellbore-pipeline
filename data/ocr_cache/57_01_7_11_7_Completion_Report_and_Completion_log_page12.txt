
D. ULA FORMATION & SMITH BANK FORMATION

D.1 ROCK DATA

This is the zone with the most potential. It includes the Jurassic and a Triassic section. The CPI (Append. 2) indicated that there was good porosity (avg. 15%), water saturation 30-60%, and a low shale content of approximately 10%. The RFT results were again indicating relatively tight rock. Core analyses (GECO Append. 3 and Fig.6) results were also very encouraging with Sw 5-10% and porosity 15-20% over the perforated interval. The corrected permeability is still low, but shows a large spread from .01 to 10 md. This plotted permeability correlates with the porosity which is a negative indicator for fracture permeability. This correlation also points out the necessity of taking care in the stimulation procedure not to occlude the pore throats by precipitating fines or relocating clays. This section was cored, but the most important analyses of these cores were not received until after the well was plugged.

D.2 SERVICE COMPANY DESIGNS

Partial core analysis was done by Dowell-Schlumberger and Halliburton. The Dowell-Schlumberger report (Append. 16) matches the CPI 5-13% porosity, 3-4 md permeability in those samples above the flow meter threshold level, and a mineralogy of 95-99% quartz with minor traces of illite was noted. Their work on solubilities indicated that the formation was mildly soluble (4-13%) in 15% HCl, but very soluble in 12-3 Mud Acid (12% HF 3% HCl) as up to 41% of the rock would dissolve. This high percentage is not consistent with their analysed mineralogy. The increase in permeability to seawater was a factor of eight after treatment with HF. They noted no swelling of the illite and so discounted problems with the clays. Halliburton (Append. 17), with sample splits from the same depths as Dowell-Schlumberger, generated very different results. They noted only 40-65% quartz and anywhere from 6-30% clays. The permeability measurements were .45-5.6 md with high porosity 13-16%. There was again no sensitivity or fines flows noted for various fluids. They did include an actual flow capacity vs. closure pressure test for the ceramic proppant and bauxite proppant which has been plotted on Fig. 12 along with the idealized proppant curves. It is readily apparent that for closure pressures up to 10,000 psi the lighter density ceramic (interprop) yields a better flow capacity. The calculated closure pressure at this depth is less than 8,000 psi.
