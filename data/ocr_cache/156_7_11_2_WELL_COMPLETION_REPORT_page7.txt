# GEOLOGY

## Regional Geology

The Phillips 7/11-2X well on the Cod prospect is located in the deepest part of the Norwegian Tertiary Basin which trends north-northwesterly through Norwegian, Danish and Dutch Waters. The axis of the basin closely approximates the median line between Great Britain and Norway toward which both structural rise and stratigraphic thinning of the Tertiary occur. In the central basin in the vicinity of the well, the Tertiary section may be up to 12,000 feet in thickness.

## Prospect Geology

The 7/11-2X well was drilled on the west flank of the north-west-trending Cod anticline 1.8 kilometers due west of the 7/11-1X well. The structure was mapped by reflection seismic surveys in 1965-66 and in 1967. It is well defined from Permian levels up through most of the Tertiary section, but the size diminishes slightly with depth.

A fault due to salt movement cuts the anticline passing centrally north-to northeastward and is scissored, breaking into paired radial faults on the north and south. It offsets each of three seismic mapping horizons with displacements averaging .020 milliseconds or 65 - 75 feet. Seismically mapped closure on the prospect, before drilling was as follows:

| Seismic Horizon: | Assumed Stratigraphic Horizon: | Area of Closure: | Vertical Closure at Loc: |
|------------------|-------------------------------|------------------|-------------------------|
| "Green"          | Near Base Tertiary - top Upper Cret. Chalk | 12.3 sq.mi. | 600' |
| "Yellow"         | Base Upper Cretaceous Chalk | 7.0 sq.mi. | 550' |
| "Vermilion"      | Top Permian Zechstein Salt | 7.2 sq.mi. | 1100' |

The Cod 7/11-1X drilled 975 feet of Paleocene section including 670 feet of Upper Paleocene sand and shale lithology and 305 feet of Lower Paleocene Danian limestone-sandstone-shale section. In the Upper Paleocene 230 feet of net pay was tested. In Cod 7/11-2X Upper Paleocene hydrocarbon-bearing section was expected to be 30 feet more than in 7/11-1X. The top of the Paleocene was expected to be 260 feet lower.
