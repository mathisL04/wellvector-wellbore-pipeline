The Jurassic section had been exposed to drilling fluids for 45 days and the necessity of cleaning the near well bore becomes greater when the drilling program is examined. PPCO's Sed-Pet study (Append. 4) shows that the Jurassic had an average of 14% clay (range 9-23%) of which 90% was illite. Illite is a clay long infamous for swelling when it is in contact with fresher water. British Petroleum sent an analysis of Upper Jurassic Ula formation water (Append. 20) which shows a chloride content of 153,025 mg/l which is approximately $140,000 \mathrm{ppm}$. The drilling program (Append. 21) indicates that this section was to be drilled with sea water (chlorides 20,000- $35,000 \mathrm{ppm}$ ) but that dilution will be made with fresh drillwater. According to the Daily Drilling Reports (Append. 31) when drilling out of the casing, the mud had a chloride content of $23,500 \mathrm{ppm}$. These chlorides decreased with time and stabilized at about 15,000-17,000 ppm. On 28 May, bentonite which is hydrated with fresh water, was added to the mud and the chlorides restabilized at $12,000-13,000 \mathrm{ppm}$. The well had started taking fluid and yielding gas while the chloride count slipped to 8,000 ppm by 9 June. On 15 June, the mud weight was increased from 13.5 to $13.7 \mathrm{ppg}$ and the chlorides dropped to $4,000 \mathrm{ppm}$. When the mud weight was increased again the following day to $13.9 \mathrm{ppg}$, the chlorides dropped still further to $2,000 \mathrm{ppm}$. When the mud finally reached $14.1 \mathrm{ppg}$, the chlorides were down to $1,500 \mathrm{ppm}$, or essentially fresh water. For twelve days the chlorides stayed below $2,000 \mathrm{ppm}$ and on only seven days of the next thirty-nine days did the chloride count ever get above $3,000 \mathrm{ppm}$.

This major drop in chloride count may have caused major damage to the formation permeability by illite swelling in the pore throats. This problem may have been exacerbated by the stimulation. While stimulating, the well head pressure was approximately $9,500 \mathrm{psi}$ and the hydrostatic head with additives at the perforations was approximately $8,000 \mathrm{psi}$. Given that the formation pressure is $11,300 \mathrm{psi}$, the difference between the treating and formation pressures is about $6,200 \mathrm{psi}$ into the formation. When the well was opened and only a column of seawater was in the tubing (hydrostatic head $6,200 \mathrm{psi}$), the pressure difference in the direction of the wellbore was only $5,100 \mathrm{psi}$. This lower pressure, coupled with a swollen, possibly mobile clay could generate a check valve effect where it is possible to pump into the formation through the clay with a differential pressure of $6,200 \mathrm{psi}$, but not possible to for the formation to return fluids against the closed clays with a differential pressure of only $5,100 \mathrm{psi}$.
